[ ROOTS ]

I was raised in a family rooted in the belief that education is the only path to curating your own freedom, and the highest form of religious devotion.

Baba (a whole finance bro fr) left corporate life to build something of his own with no safety net (where do you think I get my fearlessness from?). Ammu, a master's degree holder, chose to make our home the foundation her children would launch from (obviously I inherited her grit). Beloved extended family also had hopes for me to be the one kid out of twenty-something cousins to study abroad. They enrolled me in an English-curriculum school in 3rd grade with one conviction: little Nuju would go further.

[ REBELLION ]

Listen bro, I stg I've been drawing since I could hold a pencil, so I had to see it through. I was drawing painting doodling sketching ALL NIGHT as a kid. I chased creative pursuits (obv my heart was not in the classroom), became the school photographer for all athletic tournaments, painted a mural for our basketball court too.

And then Basketball found me despite every obstacle. In Bangladesh, women playing sports often face resistance: it's considered unsafe without constant supervision. I played anyway, duh. Sneaking out to courts. Staying longer than I should have.

At sixteen and 5'3", I played for Bangladesh's first U18 women's national basketball team.
At 16, I also had my first art exhibit.

[ THE LEAP ]

A gap year after high school ‚Äî teaching art to kindergarteners and playing national basketball, giving my family time. Then, scholarship offers from American universities.

My parents wanted me to go to New York City ‚Äî the only state with some sense of Bangladeshi community, cuisine, familiarity. I chose Rochester Institute of Technology... without knowing that it's actually 8 hours north of NYC.

60% scholarship for art, basketball, and merit. I arrived wanting to build technical solutions for people in developing countries like my own.
